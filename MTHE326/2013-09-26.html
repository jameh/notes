Differentiation as a function from \(\Bbb R^2 \to \Bbb R^2\)¶

Example

\(f(z)=\bar z\) is not complex differentiable anywhere.

\[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}=\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\bar h}{h}\]

which does not exist.

\(h\) can approach zero many ways.

Let \(h\) go to zero along the real axis. ie.e

\[h(t)=t\]

So, \(h\to 0\) as \(t\to 0\). \(t \in \Bbb R\).

then the limit simplifies:

\[\lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(z_0+t)-f(z_0)}{t}=\lim{t}{t}=1\]

Let h go to zero along the imaginary axis now.

\[h(t) = it\]

for \(t \in \Bbb R\).

then the limit simplifies to:

\[\lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(z_0+it)-f(z_0)}{it} = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{\bar{it}}{it} = -1\]

So the limit is not unique, therefore does not exist.

Proposition 6

Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are analytic on the open set \(S\). Then,

  1. \(f+g\) is analytic
\((f+g)' = f' + g'\)
  1. \(fg\) is analytic
\((fg) = f'g + fg'\)
  1. \(\frac{f}{g}\) is analytic (when \(g\neq 0\))
\((\frac{f}{g})' = \frac{gf' - g'f}{g^2}\)

Proof: same as real case.

Corollary 2

Polynomials and rational functions are analytic when denominator is not zero.

If

\[f(z)=a_nz^n + ... + a_0\]

then:

\[f'(z)=a_nnz^{n-1} + ... + a_1\]

same as real polynomials.

Proposition 7

Chain Rule

Suppose that

\[f: S_1 \to \Bbb C\]

is analytic and

\[g: S_2 \to \Bbb C\]

is also analytic, where \(S1,S2\) are open sets.

If \(f(S_1) \subseteq S_2\), then

\[g(f): S_1 \to \Bbb C\]

is analytic, and

\[(g(f(z)))' = g'(f(z))f'(z)\]

Proof: see calculus.

Digression

Let’s consider all linear functions from \(\Bbb R^2 \to \Bbb R^2\) that preserve length.

What do these look like?

  • rotations
  • reflections

Suppose that

\[T: \Bbb R^2 \to \Bbb R^2\]

preserves length. Then,

\[||Tv||=||v||\]

for all \(v \in \Bbb R^2\).

We also have a formula for the dot product.

\[v_1 \cdot v_2 = \frac{1}{2}(||v_1+v_2||^2-||v_1||^2-||v_2||^2)\]

So,

\[T(v_1) \cdot T(v_2) = \frac{1}{2}(||T(v_1)+T(v_2)||^2-||T(v_1)||^2-||T(v_2)||^2)\]\[(T(v_1) + T(v_2) = T(v_1+v_2))\]\[T(v_1) \cdot T(v_2) = \frac{1}{2}(||v_1+v_2||^2-||v_1||^2-||v_2||^2 =v_1 \cdot v_2\]

So, if it preserves length, it preserves angles between two vectors.

\[\cos\theta = \frac{v_1 \cdot v_2}{||v_1||\cdot||v_2||}\]\[= \frac{T v_1 \cdot T v_2}{||Tv_1||\cdot||Tv_2||}\]\[= \cos \phi\]

If \(f: S \to \Bbb C\) where \(S \subseteq \Bbb C\), then we can think of it as a function from \(\Bbb R^2 \to \Bbb R^2\).

\[z = x+iy\]\[= (x,y)\]

where \(x,y \in \Bbb R\).

\[f(x+iy) = u(x,y)+iv(x,y)\]

So, \(u\) is the real part of \(f\) and \(v\) is the imaginary part of \(f\).

Example

\[f(z) = z^2\]\[f(x+iy)= (x+iy)^2\]\[=(x^2-y^2)+i(2xy)\]

Define the associated function

\[F: \Bbb R^2 \to \Bbb R^2\]\[F(x,y)=(u(x,y),v(x,y))\]

That is the pair of \(u\) and \(v\).
