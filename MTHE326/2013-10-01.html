Proof of Cauchy-Riemann Equations and Conformality¶

Proof of Cauchy-Riemann Theorem¶

  1. \("\implies"\)

\(f\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\)

iff

\[\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}\]

exists.

This limit exists no matter how \(h\) approaches 0.

Let \(h=t\) where \(t \in \Bbb R\)

\[\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}\]\[= \frac{f(z_0+t)-f(z_0)}{t}\]

and since

\[f(z_0)=f(x_0+iy_0)=u(x_0,y_0)+iv(x_0,y_0)\]

then

\[\lim_{t\to 0} \frac{u(x_0+t,y_0)+iv(x_0+t,y_0)-(u(x_0,y_0)+iv(x_0,y_0))}{t}\]\[= \lim_{t\to 0} \frac{u(x_0+t,y_0)-u(x_0,y_0)}{t}+\lim_{t\to 0}i\frac{v(x_0+t,y_0)-v(x_0,y_0)}{t}\]\[= \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+i\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\]

cool. Now let’s approach along the imaginary axis!

Let \(h=it\) where \(t \in \Bbb R\).

then

\[\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}\]\[\lim_{t\to 0} \frac{u(x_0,y_0+t)+iv(x_0,y_0+t)-(u(x_0,y_0)+iv(x_0,y_0))}{t}\]\[= \lim_{t\to 0} -i\frac{u(x_0,y_0+t)-u(x_0,y_0)}{t}+\lim_{t\to 0}\frac{v(x_0,y_0+t)-v(x_0,y_0)}{t}\]\[=-i\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}(x_0,y_0)+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x_0,y_0)\]

So, equating the real and imaginary parts of the two results,

\[-\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}(x_0,y_0)=\frac{\partial v(x_0,y_0)}{\partial x(x_0,y_0)}\]

and

\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x_0,y_0)=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\]

2) We need to now proove that

\[F(x,y)=(u(x,y),v(x,y))\]

is differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\)

Recall what it means for

\[F: S\subseteq \Bbb R \to \Bbb R\]

to be differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\).

It means that there exists a 2-by-2 matrix, \(DF_{(x_0,y_0)}\) such that:

\[\lim_{(h_x,h_y)\to (0,0)} \frac{||F(x_0+h_x,y_0+h_y)-F(x_0,y_0)-DF_{(x_0,y_0)}(h_x,h_y)||}{||(h_x,h_y)||}=0\]

where \(||\cdot ||\) is the norm on \(\Bbb R^2\), i.e.

\[||(h_x,h_y)||=\sqrt{h_x^2+h_y^2}\]

If the Cauchy-Riemann Equations hold, then \(DF_{(x_0,y_0)}\) can be represented by a complex number.

Call \(w=DF_{(x_0,y_0)}\)

and \(DF_{(x_0,y_0)}(h_x,h_y)=wh\)

So we can equivalently rewrite the limit as:

\[\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{|f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)-wh|}{|h|}=0\]\[\iff \lim_{h\to 0}\left|\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)-wh}{h}\right|=0\]\[\iff lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)-wh}{h}=0\]\[\iff lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}-w=0\]\[\iff lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}=w\]

Example

Where is \(f(z)=|z|^2\) differentiable?

\[f(x+iy) =x^2+y^2\]\[u(x,y)=x^2+y^2\]\[v(x,y)=0\]

Then, the Jacobian is

\[\begin{split}\left[\matrix{ 2x & 2y \\ 0 & 0 }\right]\end{split}\]

CREs only hold at \((x,y)=(0,0)\), so it is only differentiable at \((0,0)\)

Conformality¶

Let

\[f(z)=\frac{1}{z}=\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}+i(\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2})\]\[F(x,y)=(\frac{1}{z}=\frac{x}{x^2+y^2},(\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}))\]\[\begin{split}DF=\frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\left[\matrix{ y^2-x^2 & -2xy \\ 2xy & y^2-x^2 }\right]\end{split}\]

Consider the curves:

\[\gamma_1(t)=[t,1]\]\[\gamma_2(t)=[2,s]\]\[t,s \in \Bbb R\]

let \(t=2, s=1\)

What angle do \(F(\gamma_1(t))\) and \(F(\gamma_2(t))\) make at \(F(2,1)\)?
