Proof of Cauchy’s Theorem (version 2, 3) and Homotopy¶

Recall

If \(f: D\to\mathbb C\) is holomorphic and \(γ_0,γ_1\) are simple, closed curves oriented the same way

\[\begin{split}γ_0,γ_1\subset D && γ_1\subset Int(γ_0)\cap D\\ Int(γ_1)-Int(γ_0)\subset D\end{split}\]

(see shitty image from last time)

then:

\[\int_{γ_0}f(z)dz=\int_{γ_1}f(z)dz\]

Proof of Cauchy’s Theorem (version 2)¶

Consider the simple curve, \(\tilde γ\), containing \(γ_0\) and \(γ_1\)

Claim:¶

\[\int_{γ_0}+\int_{\tilde γ}+\int_{-γ_1}+\int_{-\tilde γ}\]

We use green’s theorem (as in Cauchy’s Theorem version 1) to relate the integral around the boundary of \(R\) to an integral on \(R\)

Definition We say the loops \(γ_0\) and \(γ_1\) are homotopic within a domain \(D\) if there exists a continuous, differentiable mapping:

\[H:[0,1]\times[0,1]\to D\]

such that:

H(0,t)=γ_0(t) && H(1,t)=γ_1(t)\ H(s,0)=H(s,1)

Note

The in-between curves defined by \(H\) mustn’t leave \(D\) at any point.

Example

Let \(γ_0=5\cos 2πt+i\sin 2πt && t\in[0,1]\)

And \(γ_1=3\cos 2πt+i\sin 2πt && t\in[0,1]\)

\[H(s,t)=(5-2s)e^{2πit}\]

Cauchy’s Theorem (version 3)¶

If \(f:D\to \mathbb C\) is holomorphic and \(γ_0\) and \(γ_1\) are closed loops in \(D\) which are homotopic in \(D\), then:

\[\int_{γ_0}f(z)dz=\int_{γ_1}f(z)dz\]

In version 1, \(γ\) was a simple closed curve and \(Int(γ)\subset D\)
