Uniform Convergence and Analytic Functions, Power Series¶

Theorem (Weirstrass)

\[f_n\to f\text{ L.U. on }D\]

and \(f_n\) are holomorphic on \(D\), then so is \(f\) and

\[f_n^{(K)}\to f_n^{(K)}\text{ L.U. on }D\]

Proof:

Fix \(z_0\in D\), \(\overline{D_ε(z_0)}\) a small disk around \(z_0\) contained in \(D\).

Let \(γ=δD_ε(z_0)\)

By Cauchy’s Integral Formula:

\[\begin{split}f_n(z_0)&=\frac{1}{2πi}\int_γ\frac{f_n(w)}{w-z_0}dw\\ f(z_0)&=\frac{1}{2πi}\int_γ\frac{f(w)}{w-z_0}dw\end{split}\]

since if \(f_n(w)\to f(w)\) uniformly on \(\overline{D_ε(z_0)}\) then

\[\frac{f_n(w)}{w-z_0}\to\frac{f(w)}{w-z_0}\]

uniformly on \(γ\).

So for all \(z_0\in D\), \(f(z_0)\) is given by an integral of Cauchy type, thus analytic.

Since

\[\frac{k!}{2πi}\int_γ\frac{f_n(z)}{(w-z)^{k+1}}dz\to \frac{k!}{2πi}\int_γ\frac{f(z)}{(w-z)^{k+1}}dz\]

it is easy to show uniform convergence for the derivative.

Distinction Between Uniformly Convergent and Uniformly Convergent on Compact Sets¶

For \(z\in D_1(0)\), \(f_n(z)=z^n\to 0\) uniformly on compact sets, but not uniformly

because \(\forall 1>ε>0\), \(\forall n\in\mathbb N\) we can find a \(z\in D_1(0)\) s.t. \(|z^n=0|>ε\). Just look near the boundary.

But if \(k\subset D_1(0)\) is compact, then it is contained in some \(\overline D_δ(1)\) where \(δ<1\) so \(\forall z\in K\),

\[\begin{split}|z^n-0|<|z|^n\leq δ^n\to 0\text{ as }n\to\infty\end{split}\]

Definition (Power Series)

A power series centered at \(z_0\) is an expression of the form:

\[\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(z-z_0)^n\]

\(a_n\in\mathbb C\)

Theorem (Radius of Convergencec)

Given a power series, there exists an \(R\) s.t.

  1. if \(|z-z_0|<R\), the series converges.
  2. if \(|z-z_0|>R\), the series diverges.

Furthermore, convergence is locally uniform on \(D_R(z_0)\).

Proof:

Same proofs as real analysis, the modulus just means more now. See Section 5.4 of Textbook if curious.
