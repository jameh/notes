Cauchy’s Theorem (Version 1)¶

Suppose \(D\) is a domain and

\[f: D\to \mathbb C\]

is holomorphic on \(D\). If \(\gamma\subset D\) is a simple closed curve and its whole interior is in \(D\), then:

\[\int_\gamma f(z)dz=0\]

Non-Example

\[\begin{split}γ: |z|=1\\ f(z)=\frac{1}{z}\\ \int_γ f(z)dz \neq 0\end{split}\]

since the domain of analyticity does not contain \(z=0\) which is an interior point of the curve \(γ\).

Corollary 1

If \(f\) is holomorphic on the disk \(D_p(z_0)\), \(z_0\in\mathbb C\), \(ρ>0\), then \(f\) has an anti-derivative on \(D_ρ(z_0)\).

In the proof of the equivalence theorm, we defined:

\[F(z+h)-F(z)=\int_{γ_h} F(w)dw\]

\(F\) was anti-derivative

Let \(γ_z\) be a straight path from \(z_0\) to \(z\) and let

\[F(z)=\int_{γ_z}f(w)dw\]

where \(γ_z\) is a fixed path.

For \(h\in \mathbb C\) s.t. \(|h|\) is “small”,

Let \(\tilde γ_h\) be the straight path from \(z\) to \(z+h\)

\[γ_z+\tilde γ_h-γ_{z+h}\]

So,

\[\int_{γ_z+\tilde γ_h-γ_{z+h}} f(w)dw=0\]

by Cauchy’s Theorem.

So,

\[\begin{split}\int_{γ_z} f(w)dw+\int_{\tilde γ_h} f(w)dw-\int_{-γ_{z+h}} f(w)dw=0\\ \int_{\tilde γ_h}&=\int_{-γ_{z+h}} f(w)dw-\int_{γ_z} f(w)dw\\ &=F(z+h)-F(z)\end{split}\]

From here, repeat Proof of Equivalence Theorem.

Corollary 2

If \(f\) is an analytic function on \(D_ρ(z_0)\), \(z_0\in\mathbb C\), \(ρ>0\), and \(γ:[a,b]\to D_ρ(z_0)\), \(γ(a)=γ(b)\) (not necessarily simple), then:

\[\int_γ f(w)dw=0\]

There exists an antiderivative of \(f\) on the disk, by previous Corollary, and:

\[\int_γ f(w)dw=F(γ(b))-F(γ(a))=0\]

by FTCC

Cauchy’s Theorem (version 2)¶

Suppose \(γ_1, γ_2\) are simple closed curves in the domain \(D\) and \(γ_1\) can be continuously deformed - this term to be later defined - to \(γ_2\) (see shitty picture)

and \(γ_1,γ_2\) are oriented the same way, then:

\[\int_{γ_1}f(w)dw=\int_{γ_2}f(w)dw\]

Example

Let \(f(z)=\frac{1}{z}+\frac{1}{z-1}\)

Find \(\int_γ f(w)dw\) where:

Firstly,

\[\int_γ f(w)dw=\int_γ \frac{1}{w}dw+\int_γ \frac{1}{w-1}dw\]
\[\int_γ \frac{1}{w}dw=0\]

by Cauchy’s Theorem (version 1)

\[\int_γ \frac{1}{w-1}dw=\int_{\tilde γ} \frac{1}{w-1}dw=2πi\]

where \(\tilde γ\) is a circle around \(z=1\) oriented counter-clockwise

Aside

Partial Fractions

How to integrate something like:

\[\int_γ \frac{1}{z^2+5z+6}dz\]

by the fundamental theorem of Algebra, any polynomial of the form:

\[a_nz^n+...+a_1z+a_0\]

factors in \(\mathbb C\)

\[=a_n(z-α_1)^{m_1}\cdot ...\cdot (z-α_k)^{m_k}\]

Using partial fractions is just the technique of letting

\[\frac{1}{a_n(z-α_1)^{m_1} ... (z-α_k)^{m_k}}=\sum_{l=1}^{k}\sum_{m=0}^{m_1} \frac{A_{1,m}}{(z-α_l)^m}\]

Example

\[\begin{split}\frac{1}{z^2+36}&=\frac{1}{(z+6i)(z-6i)}\\ &=\frac{A}{(z+6i)}+\frac{B}{(z-6i)}\end{split}\]
