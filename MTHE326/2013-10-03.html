Conformatlity & Harmonic Functions¶

Last time:

\[f(z) = \frac{1}{z}\]\[F(x,y)=(\frac{x}{x^2+y^2},\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2})\]\[\begin{split}DF=\frac{1}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\left[\matrix{ y^2-x^2 & -2xy \\ 2xy & y^2-x^2 }\right]\end{split}\]

Consider the curves:

\[\gamma_1(t)=[t,1]\]\[\gamma_2(s)=[2,s]\]

At what angle do \(F(\gamma_1(t))\) and \(F(\gamma_2(s))\) meet at \(F(2,1)\)?

\[F(\gamma_1(t))=[\frac{t}{t^2+1},\frac{-1}{t^2+1}]\]\[\implies x^2+(y+\frac{1}{2})^2=\frac{1}{4}\]

and

\[F(\gamma_2(s))=[\frac{2}{4+s^2},\frac{-s}{4+s^2}]\]\[\implies (x-\frac{1}{4})^2+y^2=\frac{1}{16}\]
\[\frac{d}{dt}F(\gamma_1(t)) |_{t=2}=[\frac{1-t^2}{(1+t^2)^2},\frac{2t}{(1+t^2)^2}] |_{t=2}\]\[=[\frac{-3}{25},\frac{4}{25}]\]

and

\[\frac{d}{ds}F(\gamma_2(s)) |_{s=1}=[\frac{2}{4+s^2},\frac{-s}{4+s^2}] |_{s=1}\]\[=[\frac{-4}{25},\frac{-3}{25}]\]

So the angle between \(\gamma_1(t)\) and \(gamma_2(s)\) is preserved by \(F\) (vectors are orthogonal.)

Explain!

\[\gamma_1(t)=[t,1], \gamma_2(t)=[2,s]\]\[\frac{d}{dt}\gamma_1(t)=[1,0]\]\[\frac{d}{ds}\gamma_2(s)=[0,1]\]\[\begin{split}DF_{(2,1)}=\frac{1}{25}\left[\matrix{ -3 & -4 \\ 4 & -3 }\right]\end{split}\]

The property that a function preserves angles between curves is called conformality.

All complex-differentiable functions are conformal at \(z_0\) if the \(f'(z_0)\neq 0\).

Harmonic Functions¶

If \(S\subseteq \mathbb R^2\), open subset,

A function \(\phi: S\to\mathbb R^2\) is harmonic if \(\phi\) is twice differentiable and

\[\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial y^2}=0\]

These arise naturally in physical problems.

Examples

  • Electrostatic Potential of static charges

  • Potential function of an incompressible fluid

  • Temperature of a slab at equilibrium

Why?!

Harmonic functions usually arise from conservative vector fields. These arise from symmetry.

Section 2.6 in Textbook

Theorem

If

\[f: S\to\mathbb C\]

is analytic and

\[f(x+iy)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)\]

then \(u\) and \(v\) are harmonic.

Proof:

Assuming a fact we will prove later,

If \(f\) is analytic, \(u\) and \(v\) are infinitely differentiable.

\(f\) is analytic, so:

\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\]

and

\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\]

then, differentiating again,

\[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial y\partial x}\]

and

\[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=-\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x\partial y}\]

and because \(v\) is infinitely differentiable,

\[\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x\partial y}=\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial y\partial x}\]

So,

\[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=-\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}\]\[\implies \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=0\]
