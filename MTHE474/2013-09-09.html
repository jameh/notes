Information Theory¶

  • mathematical theory of communication
  • provides fundamental limits on how to measure/quantify/process/code information
    • providing fundamental performance bounds on communication systems (asymptotic limits)

Source Coding / Data Compression¶

Lossless vs. Lossy

Definition

Entropy: smallest compression rate for lossless compression

Read: Fundamentals of Channel coding (Shannon 1948) on capacity.

Definition

Capacity, \(C\) of a discrete channel is given by:

\[C = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{\log N(T)}{T}\]

Where \(N(T)\) is the number of allowed signals of duration T

Motivation:¶

Self-Information:

Let \(E\) be an event with probability \(p\) (\(0 \leq p \leq 1\)).

Let \(I(p)\) [or \(I(E)\)], called the self-information of event \(E\), represent the amount of information you gain when you learn that \(E\) has occured. Equivalently, the amount of uncertainty you had prior to learning that \(E\) had happened.

What are the properties \(I(p)\) should have?

  1. \(I(p)\) is a decreasing function of \(p\)
    • this makes sense intuitively: the more likely an event, the less information you gain from learning that it happens.
  2. \(I(p)\) is continuous in \(p\)
  3. If \(E_{1}\), \(E_{2}\) are independent events, with respective probabilities \(p_{1}\), \(p_{2}\),
\[I(E_{1} \cap E_{2}) = I(E_{1}) + I(E_{2}) = I(p_{1}) + I(p_{2}) = I(p_{1} \bullet p_{2})\]
