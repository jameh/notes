Entropy¶

Theorem

The only function defined over \(p \in [0,1]\) satisfying the three desired properties from last lecture is:

\[I(p) = -c \log_b p\]

for a proof, see online lecture notes Theorem 2.1

Note

The choice of constant \(c\) and base \(b\) of the logarithm just determine units (recall \(\log_b u = \frac{\ln u}{\ln b}\))

So, we set \(c=1\) and define:

Units of information
b units
2 bits
e nats

unless specified, we assume that \(b=2\).

Hence, if you learn that:

  • a fair coin comes up heads:

    \[-\log_2(\frac{1}{2}) = 1 \text{ bit}\]

  • a fair die comes up 5, you’ve gained:

    \[-\log_2(\frac{1}{6}) = 2.585 \text{ bits of information}\]

For the first part of the course, we will deal with discrete time (discrete valued) systems

Notation

Let \(X\) be a discrete random variable with finite alphabet \(\mathcal X\) and pmf \(p(x):=P[X=x]\) for \(x \in \mathcal X\) or \(p_X(a):=P[X=a]\) for \(a \in \mathcal X\).

Definition

Entropy:

The entropy of a discrete random variable \(X\) with alphabet \(\mathcal X\) and pmf \(p_X(.)\) is denoted by \(H(X)\) or \(H(p_X)\) or \(H(p)\) and defined by:

\[H(X) := -\sum_{x \in \mathcal X} p(x) log_2 p(x)\]

Note

\(H(X) = \sum_{x \in \mathcal X} p(x) (-\log_2 p(x))\)

where the summand is the product of \(p(x)\) and the self information of event \(X=x\).

Thus, the entropy of a system is the expected value of self information for the system of events.

\[H(X) = E_p[I(X)]\]

where

\[I(x) = -\log_2 p(x)\]

Therefore, \(H(X)\) is the statistical average amount of information gained when you learn that one of the \(|\mathcal X|\) outcomes of \(X\) has occured.

Lemma

  1. \(H(X)\) is non-negative

    \[H(X) \geq 0\]
  2. \[H_b(X):= -\sum_{x \in \mathcal X} p(x) log_b p(x) = log_b 2 H(X)\]

    where \(H(X)\) is in base 2.

Example

Binary Entropy function:

\[\mathcal X = \{0,1\}, 0 \leq p \leq 1\]\[P[X=0]=1-p\]\[P[X=1]=p\]\[H(X) = \sum_{x=0}^1 = p(x) log_2 p(x)\]\[= -p log_2 p -(1-p) log_2 (1-p) =: h(p)\]

The fundamental inequality (FI)¶

For the logarithm,

\[\ln x \leq x-1 \forall x \gt 0\]

with equality if and only if \(x=1\).

Corollary

\[\ln y \geq 1 - \frac{1}{y}\]

with equality iff \(x=1\)

Proof: let \(y=\frac{1}{x}\)

So, two useful forms of the Fundamental Inequality are:

\[\log_a x \leq \frac{1}{\ln a} (x-1)\]\[\log_a x \geq \frac{1}{\ln a} (1 - \frac{1}{x})\]

Definition

Joint Entropy

Given two jointly distributed discrete random variables \(X\) and \(Y\), (with alphabets \(\mathcal X\) and \(Y\)) with join pmf

\[p_{XY}(x,y) = p(x,y) = P(X=x, Y=y)\]

where \(x \in \mathcal X\) and \(y \in Y\)

Their joint entropy, denoted \(H(X,Y)\) is:

\[H(X,Y) := E_{XY}[-log p(X,Y)]\]\[= -\sum_{x \in \mathcal X} \sum_{y \in Y} p(x,y) \log_2 p(x,y)\]

Definition

Conditional Entropy

The conditional entropy of random variable \(X\) given \(Y\) is

\[H(X|Y) := E_{XY}[-log p(X|Y)]\]\[= - \sum_{x \in \mathcal X} \sum_{y \in Y} p(x,y) \log_2 p(x|y)\]

where

\[p(x|y) = \frac{p(x,y)}{p(y)}\]
