Results from Number Theory (contd)¶

Let \(b_1,...,b_r\) be a finite set of positive integers with gcd 1. Then there exists integers \(a_1,...,a_r\) (not necessarily non-negative) such that \(a_1b_1+...+a_rb_r=1\)

Proof¶

Consider the set of all integers of the form

\[c_1b_1+...c_rb_r\]

where the \(c_i\) range over the integers.

Let \(l\) denote the least positive integer in this set. We will show that \(l=1\). It suffices to show that \(l\) divides all of \(b_1,...b_r\). We will show \(l\) divides \(b_i\) by proof by contradiction.

Suppose \(l\) does not divide \(b_i\). Then

\[b_i=ql+R\]

where \(q\geq 0\) is an integer and \(0<R<l\).

But then

\[\begin{split}R&=b_i-ql \\ &=b_i-q(a_1b_1+...+a_rb_r)\end{split}\]

(let \(l=a_1b_1+...+a_rb_r\))

\[=(1-a_iq)b_i+\sum_{j\neq 1}(-qa_j)b_j\]

Since \(R<l\), this contradicts the minimality of \(l\).

Let \(b_1,...,b_r\) be a finite collection of positive integers with gcd 1. Then there exists a positive integer \(M\) such that for every \(n>M\), there exist nonnegative integers \(d_1,...,d_r\) such that \(n=d_1b_1+...+d_rb_r\).

Proof¶

From previous proof, there exist integers (not necessarily nonnegative) \(a_1,...,a_r\) such that \(a_1b_1+...+a_rb_r=1\). Now choose

\[M=(|a_1|b_1+...+|a_r|b_r)b_1\]

If \(n>M\), then we can write:

\[n=M+qb_1+R\]

where \(q\geq 0\) is an integer and \(0\leq R<b_1\) is an integer.

If \(R=0\), then we are done, since we can take \(d_1=|a_1|b_1+...+|a_r|b_r+q\) and \(d_k=0\) for \(k=2,...,r\)

If \(0<R<b_1\), then

\[\begin{split}n&=M+qb_1+R\times 1 \\ &=M+qb_1+R(a_1b_1+...+a_rb_r)\\ &=(|a_1|b_1+q+Ra_1)b_1+\sum_{k=2}^r(|a_k|b_1+Ra_k)b_k\end{split}\]

which is of the form desired. Since \(n\) was arbitrary, the proof is finished.

Back to Markov Chains¶

Now, suppose \(\{X_n:n\geq 0\}\) that is irreducible, positive recurrent, and aperiodic (i.e. the period of every state is 1). Consider state \(j\).

Then \(\{n:p_{ji}(n>0)\}\) has gcd 1.

By result (1), we can take a finite subset \(b_1,...,b_r\) such that \(p_{jj}(b_k)>0\) for \(k=1,...,r\) and \(gcd(b_1,...,b_r)=1\).

Take \(a_q,...,a_r\) such that \(a_1b_1+...+a_rb_r=1\) (we can do so by result (2))

and take

\[M=|a_1|b_1+...+|a_r|b_r\]

(by result (3))

Then for \(n>M\), there exist nonnegative integers \(d_1,...,d_r\) such that \(n=d_1b_1+...+d_rb_r\).

Then

\[p_{jj}(n) \leq p_{jj}(b_1)\times ...\times p_{jj}(b_1)\times ...\times p_{jj}(b_r)\times ...\times p_{jj}(b_r)\]

where the inequality follows from the fact that the RHS is the probability of a subset of the set of all paths that go from \(j\) back to \(j\) in \(n\) steps, i.e. a subset of the LHS event.

\[\begin{split}>0\end{split}\]

since \(p_{jj}(b_i)>0\) for all \(i\).

Also, if \(i\neq j\), then since the chain is irreducible, there is some \(m>0\) such that \(p_{ij}(m)>0\).

But

(1)\[p_{ij}(m+n)\geq p_{ij}(m)p_{jj}(n)\]

which is positive for all \(n\) big enough.

Let \(Π\) be the unique stationary distribution for the \(X\) chain. We wish to show that

\[p_{ij}(n)\to Π_j\text{ as }n\to\infty\text{ for any }i\]

Recall that

\[Π_j=\sum_{k\in S} Π_kp_{kj}(n)\]

Using this, we can write:

\[\begin{split}Π_j - p_{ij}(n)&=\sum_{k\in S} Π_kp_{kj}(n)-\sum_{k\in S} Π_kp_{ij}(n)\\ &=\sum_{k\in S} Π_k(p_{kj}(n)-p_{ij}(n))\end{split}\]

Take limit as \(n\to\infty\) on both sides, and show that it converges to limit 0.

note

On the RHS, we can take the limit inside by an argument similar to last lecture, by dividing our state space \(S\) into a finite set, \(F\), and its (generally infinite) complement set \(F^c\).
