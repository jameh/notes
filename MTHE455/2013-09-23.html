Markov Chains¶

Chapter 4

We will be dealing with stochastic processes with discrete state space \(S\), and index set a subset of the integers, usually \(\{0,1,2,...\}\) and having the Markov property

“I’m thinking, I’m thinking, I’m thinking, that it might be a good idea, if we dont have any idea of what anything is!!!! :DD!!”

Definition Version 1

For any \(s,i_0,i_1,...,i_{n-1} \in S\), and say \(n \geq 1\),

\[P(X_n=s|X_0=i_0,...,X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]\[= P(X_n=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]

is conditional only on recent past.

Corollary

Note that we do not have to condition on the entire past.

Say \(I' \subset \{0,1,...,n-2\}\) and consider

\[P(X_n=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1}, X_k=i_k, k \in I')\]

This probability is also equal to

\[P(X_n=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]

Proof:

\[\sum_{i\in S, k \in I', j\in I''}P(X_n=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1}, X_k=i_k,k\in I', j\in I'')\cdot P(X_j=i_j,j\in I''|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1}, X_k=i_k,k\in I')\]\[= \sum_{i_j\in S, j\in I''}P(X_n=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\cdot 1\]

Definition Version 2

For any \(s,i_0,i_1,...,i_{n-1} \in S\), and say \(n \geq 1\), and \(m \geq 0\):

\[P(X_{n+m}=s|X_0=i_0,...,X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]\[= P(X_{n+m}=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]

Note

Here we can again remove some of the events before time \(n-1\) in the conditioning, and still have the same RHS as before

Also note, that this probability possibly could reduce to

\[P(X_{n+m})\]

Proof that Version 1 \(\implies\) Version 2:¶

Proof by induction on \(m\); for \(m=0\), Version 2 is the same as Version 1. Assume Version 2 holds for \(m\). We show it holds for \(m+1\). Condition on \(X_{n+m}\).

\[P(X_{n+m+1}=s|X_0=i_0,...,X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]\[= \sum_{s'\in S}P(X_{n+m+1}=s|X_{n+m}=s',X_0=i_0,...,X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\cdot P(X_{n+m}=s'|X_0=i_0,...,X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]\[= \sum_{s'\in S}P(X_{n+m+1}=s|X_{n+m}=s',X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\cdot P(X_{n+m}=s'|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]

where the first term reduces by Version 1, and second term reduces by induction hypothesis.

\[= 1\cdot \sum_{s'\in S}P(X_{n+m+1}=s|X_{n+m}=s',X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]\[= P(X_{n+m+1}=s|X_{n-1}=i_{n-1})\]

So, Version 2 holds for Version 1.

\(\square\)

In this course, we will consider only time-homogeneous Markov chains.

i.e.

\[P(X_{n+m}=j|X_m=i)=P(X_{n+m+k}=j|X_{m+k}=i)\]

for any \(i,j \in S\), and any \(m,n,k\).

So, \(P(X_n=j|X_{n-1}=i)=P(X_1=j|X_0=i)\) because of time-homogeneity.

These are called one-step transition probabilities and denoted by \(p_{ij}\).

They are usually put into a matrix (p_{ij})_{i,jin S}

P is an \(|S|\) by \(|S|\) matrix.

P is called the transition matrix.

For any transition matrix,

\[\sum_{j\in S}p_{ij} = \sum_{j\in S}P(X_1=j|X_0=i)=1\]

Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations¶

Let \(p_{ij}(n)= P(X_n=j|X_0=i)\)

denote the \(n\) -step transition probabilities.

Note

\[p_{ij}(1)=p_{ij}\]

and let \(P(n)\) denote the \(n\) - step transion matrix whose entries are \(p_{ij}(n)\) (again an \(|S|\) by \(|S|\) matrix)

The Chapman-Kolmogorov equations are:

\[P(m+n)=P(m)P(n)\]

which is the same as

\[p_{ij}(m+n)=\sum_{k\in S}p_{ik}(m)p_{kj}(n)\]

Proof:¶

Condition on \(X_m\)

\[p_{ij}(m+n)=P(X_{m+n}=j|X_1=i)\]\[= \sum_{k\in S}P(X_{m+n}=j|X_m=k,X_0=k)P(X_m=k|X_0=i)\]\[= \sum_{k\in S}P(X_{m+n}=j|X_m=k)P(X_m=k|X_0=i)\]

by the Markov Property

\[= \sum_{k\in S}P(X_{n}=j|X_0=k)P(X_m=k|X_0=i)\]

by time-homogeneity.

\[= \sum_{k\in S}p_{kj}(n)p_{ik}(m)\]

Note

Applying the Chapman-Kolmogorov equations recursively, we get

\[P(n)=P(n-1)P(1)\]\[=P(n-2)P(1)^2\]\[=...\]\[=P(1)^n = p^n\]

So, \(p\) determines all the \(n\) - step probabilities.

Let \(X_{n_1},...X_{n_k}\) be any finite collection of random variabales in our Markov chain, with

\[\begin{split}n_1 < n_2 < ... < n_k\end{split}\]

Then,

\[P(X_{n_1}=i_1,...X_{n_k}=i_k)\]\[= P(X_{n_k}=i_k|X_{n_1}=i_1,...,X_{n_{k-1}}=i_{k-1})\cdot P(X_{n_1}=i_1,...,X_{n_{k-1}}=i_{k-1})\]\[...\]\[= P(X_{n_k}=i_k|X_{n_{k-1}}=i_{k-1})\cdot P(X_{n_{k-1}}=i_{k-1}|X_{n_{k-1}}=i_{k-2})\cdot\cdot\cdot P(X_{n_2}=i_2|X_{n_1}=i_1)\cdot P(X_{n_1}=i_1)\]

We have almost shown:

\(p\) and the initial distribution of \(X_0\) determine all finite-dimensional distributions.
