Limiting Probabilities¶

Last time:¶

\(\{X_n:n\geq 0\}\) is an irreducible, positive recurrent, and aperiodic.

\[\lim_{n\to\infty}(Π_j-p_{ij}(n))=\sum_{k\in S}Π_k\lim_{n\to\infty}(p_{kj}(n)-p_{ij}(n))\]

So our goal is to show:

\[\lim_{n\to\infty}(p_{kj}(n)-p_{ij}(n))=0\]

Basic idea:¶

Definition

Coupling: consider an independent Markov chain \(Y=\{Y_n:n\geq 0\}\) with the same transition probabiliities as \(X\) chain.

Start \(Y\) in state \(k\). Start \(X\) in state \(i\). When they reach a common state, we say they have “coupled”. After this point, they have the same distribution.

Define the bivariate process

\[Z=\{Z_n:n\geq 0\}\]

where \(Z_n=(X_n,Y_n)\) with state space \(S\times S\).

Then,

  1. \(\{Z_n: n\geq 0\}\) is a Markov chain (Markov property is inherited from \(X\) and \(Y\) chains)

  2. The transition probabilities for the \(Z\) chain are:

    \[p_{(i,k),(j.l)}=p_{ij}p_{kl}\]

    More generally, the n-step transition probabilities are

    \[p_{(i,k),(j.l)}(n)=p_{ij}(n)p_{kl}(n)\]

  3. From (2.) and our number theory results, there is an \(N_1\) such that \(p_{ij}(n)>0\) for all \(n\geq N_1\) and an \(N_2\) such that \(p_{kl}(n)>0\) for all \(n\geq N_2\).

    So for \(n\geq max(N_1,N_2)\), \(p_{ij}(n)p_{kl}(n)>0\)

    So, \(p_{ij}(n)p_{kl}(n)\) for some \(n\).

    So, \((j,l)\) is accessible from \((i,k)\). This holds for all states \((i,k)\) and \((j,l)\). So the \(Z\) chain is irreducible.

    Example

    Suppose

    \[\begin{split}P=\left[\matrix{0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0}\right]\end{split}\]

    The period is 2. Then the bivariate chain \(Z\) will not be irreducible since for example, if it starts in \((1,2)\), it will oscillate between \((1,2)\) and \((2,1)\).

    So, for example \((2,2)\) is not accessible from \((1,2)\).

  4. The stationary distribution for the \(Z\) chain is given by \(Π_{(i,k)}=Π_iΠ_k\) (where \(Π\) is the stationary distribution of \(X\) and \(Y\) chains).

    To check this:

    \[\begin{split}Π_{(j,l)}&=\sum_{(i,j)\in S\times S}Π_{(i,k)}p_{(i,k),(j,l)}\\ Π_jΠ_l&=\sum_{(i,j)\in S\times S}Π_iΠ_kp_{ij}p_{kl}\\ &=\sum_{i\in S}Π_ip_{ij}\sum_{k\in S}Π_kp_{kl}\\ &=Π_jΠ_l\end{split}\]

    Therefore, the \(Z\) chain is positive recurrent.

    So, for a state \((s,s)\), where \(s\in S\), if \(T=\) first time chain visites \((s,s)\), then \(P(T<\infty|X_0=i,Y_0=k)=1\) for any states \(i\) and \(k\).

Now consider the following probability:

\[P(X_n=j, T=m|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\]

for \(m<n\).

\[\begin{split}&=P(X_n=j|T=m,X_0=i,Y_0=k)P(T=m|X_0=i, Y_0=k)\\ &=P(X_n=j|X_m=s,Y_m=s,(X_r,Y_r)\neq (s,s), r=1,...m-1, X_0=i,Y_0=k)\\ &=P(X_n=j|X_m=s)\end{split}\]

By independence of \(X,Y\) and by Markov property of \(X\).

\[=P(Y_n=j|Y_m=s)\]

because \(Y\) has the same transition probabilities as \(X\).

\[\begin{split}&=P(Y_n=j|X_m=s,Y_m=s,(X_r,Y_r)\neq (s,s), r=1,...m-1, Y_0=i,X_0=k)\\ &=P(Y_n=j|T=m,Y_0=i,X_0=k)P(T=m|Y_0=i, X_0=k)\\ &=P(Y_n=j, T=m|Y_0=i,X_0=k)\end{split}\]

So now we have:

\[\begin{split}p_{ij}(n)&=P(X_n=j|X_0=i)\\ &=P(X_n=j|X_0=i, Y_0=k)\\ &=\sum_{m=1}^{n-1}P(X_n=j,T=m|X_0=i,Y_0=k)+P(X_n=j,T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\\ &=\sum_{m=1}^{n-1}P(Y_n=j,T=m|Y_0=i,X_0=k)+P(Y_n=j,T\geq n|Y_0=i,X_0=k)\\ &=P(Y_n=n,T<n|X_0=i,Y_0=j)+P(X_n=j,T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\\ &\leq P(Y_n=j|X_0=i,Y_0=k)+P(X_n=j,T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\\ &=P(Y_n=j|Y_0=k)+P(T\geq n| X_0=i,Y_0=k)\\ &=p_{kj}(n)+P(T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\end{split}\]

So \(p_{ij}(n)-p_{kj}(n)\leq P(T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\).

Now, interchange \(i\) and \(k\) and \(X\) and \(Y\) in the previous calculations, giving:

\[p_{kj}(n)-p_{ij}(n)\leq P(T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\]

This gives:

\[|p_{ij}(n)-p_{kj}(n)|\leq P(T\geq n|X_0=i,Y_0=k)\to 0\text{ as }n\to\infty\]

Since \(P(T<\infty|X_0=i,Y_0=k)=1\)

By the limit result just proved, one way to numerically approximate the stationary distribution \(Π\) is to take \(P,P^2,P^4,P^16\) to get \(P^n\) large enough so all the rows of \(P_n\) are close to \(Π\).

Or take \(Π^(0)\). Then compute \(Π^(1)=Π^(0)P\)

Recursively compure \(Π^(k)=Π^(k-1)P\), which for \(k\) large, goes to \(Π\)
