Theorem

For an irruducible recurrent Markov chain, a stationary distribution \(\Pi\) exists if and only if all states are positive recurrent. In this case, \(\Pi\) is unique and given by \(\Pi_i=\frac{1}{\mu_i}\) where \(\mu_i\) is the mean return time to state \(i\).

Positive recurrence is a class property:

Lemma

If \(i\leftrightarrow j\) and \(i\) is positive recurrent, then \(j\) is positive recurrent.

Consider the Markov chain whose state space are the states in the recurrent class containing \(i\) and \(j\). This is a proper Markov chain since a recurrent class is closed.

Since \(i\) is positive recurrent, there exists a stationary distribution \(\Pi\) (we showed how to construct it). Thus, all states are positive recurrent - in particular state \(j\).

Consider a transient state \(j\). This means that \(f_j<1\) where \(f_j\) is the probability of ever returning to \(j\), starting in \(j\). Let \(i\) be any other state. Then, given that the chain starts in \(i\), the number of times state \(j\) is visited is no more than

\[1+X\]

where \(X\) is the number of times the chain returns to \(j\) after the first visit. Where \(X\) has a Geometric distribution with parameter \(1-f_j\).

So the expected number of times \(j\) is visited, starting in \(i\), is upper bounded by:

\[\begin{split}1+E[x]=1+\frac{1}{1-f_j}<\infty\end{split}\]

Let

\[T_j=\text{ number of times }j\text{ is visited starting at time 1}\]

and

\[\begin{split}I_j(n) = \begin{cases} 1 &\text{ if }X_n=j \\2 &\text{ if }X_n\neq j \end{cases}\end{split}\]

So,

\[T_j=\sum_{n=1}^\infty I_j(n)\]

Then

\[E[T_j|X_0=i]\]\[=E[\sum_{n=1}^\infty I_j(n)|X_0=i]\]\[=\sum_{n=1}^\infty P(X_n=j|X_0=i)\]\[\begin{split}=\sum_{n=1}^\infty p_{ij}(n)<\infty\end{split}\]

by previous argument.

Therefore, \(p_{ij}(n)\to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\)

Note also that \(p_{jj}(n)\to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\).

Now suppose we have an irreducible, transient Markov chain. Suppose a stationary distribution \(\Pi\) did exist. Then

\[\Pi_j=P(X_n=j)=\sum_{i\in S}P(X_n=j|X_0=i)P(X_0=i)\]\[=\sum_{i\in S}p_{ij}(n)\Pi_i\]\[=\sum_{i\in F}p_{ij}(n)\Pi_i+\sum_{i\in F^c}p_{ij}(n)\Pi_i\]

where \(F\) is any finite subset of the state-space \(S\).

\[\leq \sum_{i\in F}p_{ij}(n)\Pi_i+\sum_{i\in F^c}1\cdot \Pi_i\]

Now let \(n\to \infty\). The limit can be taken inside the finite sum, and the limit is 0.

So we get

\[\Pi_j\leq \sum_{i\in F^c}\Pi_i\]

Let \(\epsilon<0\) be given.

Now choose \(F\) so that \(\sum_{i\in F^c}\Pi_i<\epsilon\)

So \(\Pi_j\leq \epsilon\). Therefore, \(\Pi_j=0\).

But \(j\) was arbitrary, so \(\Pi_j=0\) for all \(j\in S\)

This contradicts that \(\sum_{i\in S}\Pi_i=1\)

So we can restate the theorem to remove the recurrent condition:

Theorem

For an irruducible Markov chain, a stationary distribution \(\Pi\) exists if and only if all states are positive recurrent. In this case, \(\Pi\) is unique and given by \(\Pi_i=\frac{1}{\mu_i}\) where \(\mu_i\) is the mean return time to state \(i\).

  1. Let \(n_1,n_2,...\) be a sequence of positive integers with gcd 1. Then there exists a finite subset of the sequence, say \(b_1,b_2,...,b_r\) with gcd 1.

Let \(b_1=n_1\), and \(b_2=n_2\) Let \(g=gcd(b_1,b_2)\).

If \(g=1\), we are done.

If \(g>1\), let \(p_1,...,p_d\) be the distinct prime factors of \(g\) that are larger than 1. For each \(p_k\), there is at least one integer from \(\{n_3,n_4,...\}\) that \(p_k\) does not divide (Otherwise, \(p_k\) would divide all the \(n_1,n_2,...\), and \(p_k>1\), contradicting the gcd being 1).

Choose \(b_3'\) from \(\{n_3,...\}\) such that \(p_1\) does not divide \(b_3'\).

...

Choose \(b_{d+2}'\) from \(\{n_3,...\}\) such that \(p_1\) does not divide \(b_{d+2}'\).

Let \(b_3,...,b_r\) be the distinct integers among \(b_3',...,b_{d+2}'\).

Then \(\{b_1,...,b_r\}\) has gcd 1

\(p_k\) does not divide at least one of \(b_3,...,b_r\).

But \(p_1,...,p_d\) are the only divisors of \(b_1, b_2\).
